\section{MATLAB Simulation}

\subsection{Range FFT}

The goal of this module is to simulate how an FMCW radar would identify object
ranges. Initially, I planned to simulate the RF frontend: chirp generation and
mixing; this proved to be infeasible to do with realistic parameters as the
operating frequency of an FMCW radar is too large to be computed numerically.
The chosen alternative approach is to generate a realistic IF signal given an
array of ranges. This was done in the following manner.
\begin{gather*}
    \tau_i = \frac{2d_i}{c} \qquad
    f_{IF,i} = \frac{B}{T_C} \tau_i \qquad
    IF_i = \cos\left(2\pi f_{IF,i} t\right) \\
    \implies IF = \left(\sum^i IF_i\right) * \text{noise}
\end{gather*}
Taking the FFT of $IF$ results in peaks at frequencies representing objects
detected at different ranges. The object range represented by each frequency is
as follows.
\begin{equation*}
    f_{IF} = \tau \, \frac{B}{T_C} = \frac{2d}{c} \frac{B}{T_C}
    \quad \implies \quad d = \frac{fc\,T_C}{2B}
\end{equation*}
The range FFT can then be plotted as the positive half of $FFT\left(IF\right)$
vs $d$.
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{figures/range-fft.png}
    \caption{Range FFT for objects at d = $\qty{50}{\meter}, \qty{60}{\meter}, \qty{120}{\meter}$}
\end{figure}

\newpage

There are limitations on the resolution and maximum distance of range detection
that are determined by the system parameters.
\begin{gather*}
    d_{res} = \frac{c}{2B} \qquad
    d_{max} = \frac{F_s c T_C}{2B}
\end{gather*}\vspace{-1.5em}
\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{ll}
        \toprule
        \midrule
        $B$ & Chirp bandwidth \\
        $T_C$ & Chirp period \\
        $F_s$ & Sampling frequency \\
        $f_c$ & Carrier frequency \\
        $c$ & Speed of light \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}

\end{table}

\subsection{Range-Doppler FFT}
The goal of this module is to simulate how an FMCW would determine object
velocities. Firstly, we must simulate the effect of object velocities on the
objects' range from chirp to chirp. For each chirp I defined the change in
range as
\begin{equation*}
    d_{step} = v * T_C \quad \implies \quad
    d_n = d_{n-1} + d_{step}
\end{equation*}
Velocity estimation is calculated by monitoring changes in IF phase from chirp
to chirp. Therefore, it is necessary to update the representation of the IF
signal to one that reflects its true phase.
\begin{equation*}
    IF = e^{j(2\pi f_{IF} t + \varphi)} \qquad , \qquad
    \varphi = 2\pi \frac{2d}{c} f_c
\end{equation*}
The standard way to calculate the velocities based on the phases is through the
range-Doppler matrix
\begin{gather*}
    X = \begin{bmatrix}
        FFT\left(IF_1\right) \\
        FFT\left(IF_2\right) \\
        \dots \\
        FFT\left(IF_{N_c}\right) \\
    \end{bmatrix} \quad \implies \quad
    X \in \mathbb{C}^{N_c \times N} \\\\
    Y = \begin{bmatrix}
        FFT\left(X_0\right) &
        FFT\left(X_1\right) & \dots
        FFT\left(X_N\right) \\
    \end{bmatrix} \quad \implies \quad
    Y \in \mathbb{C}^{N_c \times N}
\end{gather*}
\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{ll}
        \toprule
        \midrule
        $X$ & Range profiles matrix \\
        $Y$ & Range Doppler matrix \\
        $N_c$ & Num chirps considered for $v$ estimation \\
        $N$ & Num samples for FFT \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}
\noindent $Y$ can be presented as an image where the axis are scaled using
\begin{equation*}
    d = \frac{fc\,T_C}{2B} \quad , \quad
    v = \frac{f_{doppler} c}{2f_c}
\end{equation*}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{figures/range-doppler.png}
    \caption{Range Doppler map for objects at $d = 50, \, 60, \, 120$ with $v =
    5, -10, -15$ respectively}
    \label{fig:rd1}
\end{figure}
There are limitations on the resolution and maximum velocity of velocity
estimation that are determined by the system parameters
\begin{gather*}
    v_{res} = \frac{\lambda}{2N_c T_c} \qquad v_{max} = \frac{\lambda}{4T_c} \\
\end{gather*}

\subsection{Realistic Target Modeling}

Due to the nature of simulating radar responses by generating representative IF
signals, the \\ Range-Doppler map is incredibly precise with narrow peaks and
low sidelobes (\ref{fig:rd1}). This section describes the steps taken in an
attempt to create a more realistic response.

\subsubsection{Target Spreading}

Generating an IF signal representative of a reflection at range $d$ doesn't match
what happens physically. In practice, an object at $d$ has some non-zero size
and sends back many reflections at slightly different times and phases. To
simulate this ranges can be "spread" by generating $N$ reflections per object
range.
\begin{gather*}
    \text{For } \mathbf{r_0}, \mathbf{v_0} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times 1} \\\\
    \mathbf{r} = \begin{bmatrix}
        r_0 \\
        r_0 + X_1\Delta r \\
        \dots \\
        r_0 + X_N\Delta r \\
        r_1 \\
        r_1 + X_{N + 1}\Delta r \\
        \dots \\
        r_1 + X_{2N}\Delta r \\
        \dots \\
        r_n \\
        r_n + X_{(n - 1)N + 1}\Delta r \\
        \dots \\
        r_n + X_{nN}\Delta r \\
    \end{bmatrix} \qquad
    \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix}
        v_0 \\
        v_0 + Y_1\Delta v \\
        \dots \\
        v_0 + Y_N\Delta v \\
        v_1 \\
        v_1 + Y_{N + 1}\Delta v \\
        \dots \\
        v_1 + Y_{2N}\Delta v \\
        \dots \\
        v_n \\
        v_n + Y_{(n - 1)N + 1}\Delta v \\
        \dots \\
        v_n + Y_{nN}\Delta v \\
    \end{bmatrix} \\\\
    X_i \sim \mathcal{U}(-1, 1) \qquad
    Y_i \sim \mathcal{U}(-1, 1)
\end{gather*}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.93\textwidth]{figures/spreading.png}
    \caption{Range-Doppler -- Nonspreading (left) vs Spreading (right)}
\end{figure}

This crude expansion of targets generated good visual results but lacks
physical rigour and introduced amplitude scaling issues due to constructive
interference between scatterers. The following sections assume single point
scatters per target.

\subsubsection{Swerling 2 RCS Fluctuation}

An oversight of the approach so far is that it is assumed that the return
signals from the Radar Cross Section (RCS) is non-fluctuating (i.e. for an
object a given range and velocity an identical IF signal will be generated
every time).

Swerling models II and IV match the behavior of pulse to pulse (or in our case
chirp to chirp) fluctuations. Slow fluctuations or frame to frame fluctuations
would not change the behavior of our simulation because it is currently single
frame. Between models II and IV, model II is chosen because similar intensity
reflectors is standard for most targets.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.5}
    \begin{tabular}{ccccc}
        \toprule
        Model & Decorrelation & pdf of $A$ & pdf of RCS & Geometry \\
        \midrule
        I & scan-to-scan & \multirow{2}{*}{Rayleigh} & \multirow{2}{*}{Exponential}
        & \multirow{2}{2in}{\centering Several independent reflectors of similar intensity} \\
        II & pulse-to-pulse \\
        III & scan-to-scan & \multirow{2}{*}{$4^\text{th}$ order Chi} & \multirow{2}{*}{$4^\text{th}$ order Chi-square}
        & \multirow{2}{2in}{\centering Several independent reflectors and one predominates} \\
        IV & pulse-to-pulse \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Characteristics of Swerling models}
\end{table}
The Rayleigh probability density function (pdf) is as follows
\begin{equation*}
    p(A) = \frac{2A}{\bar{\sigma}}\exp\left(-\frac{A^2}{\bar{\sigma}}\right)
\end{equation*}

    \caption{Rayleigh distribution for $\text{RCS} = 20$}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Target Detection}

\subsubsection{Cell Averaging Constant False Alarm Rate Detectors (CA-CFAR)}

Constant false alarm rate (CFAR) detectors are designed to detect objects for a
specified probability for a false alarm ($P_{F\!A}$). Compared to the
Neyman-Pearson (NP) detector, where it is assumed that the interference
environment is homogeneous (implying a constant threshold), CFAR detectors are
viable in non-homogeneous interference environments (and thus generate a
varying threshold).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.86\textwidth]{figures/cfar-architecture.png}
    \caption{CFAR detector using measured samples to derive a threshold
    designed to maintain a constant $P_{F\!A}$ in heterogeneous environments.}
\end{figure}

The figure above specifies the architecture of a 1D CFAR detector implemented
using cell averaging (CA). The CFAR window represents the segment of the data
that is being considered for the detection of the Cell Under Test (CUT). In
order to determine a decision for the CUT, a threshold is calculated from the
lagging and leading cells. Guard cells (G) are cells directly around the CUT
that are not considered when calculating the threshold; This is to avoid the
impact of multi-cell radar responses and leakage from skewing the threshold.

Near the edges of the data window where there is not enough room for $N_R/2$
lagging/leading cells, the non-constricted window can be extended by the same
amount the constricted window is shortened. Said another way: the CUT need not
always be in the center of the CFAR window.

The threshold is computed as $\alpha$ times the mean power of the reference
cells, where $\alpha$ is chosen to achieve the desired $P_{F\!A}$. And $\hat{g}$
is a function of data in the reference window (lagging and leading for 1D).
\begin{equation*}
    T = \alpha \hat{g}
\end{equation*}
The cell averaging concept is naturally expanded into two dimensions (although
the concept of lagging and leading cells is less meaningful). \\

\noindent The CA-CFAR described and implemented here asserts the following:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The interference in the reference window and CUT is independent and
        identically distributed.
    \item With a target return present in the CUT, the leading and lagging
        windows do not contain returns from other targets that bias the
        threshold estimate.
    \item The interference is Rayleigh distributed in voltage
    \item The rectifier is square law
\end{enumerate}

\noindent The best estimate of interference power of samples in the reference
window ($z = [z_1, z_2, \dots, z_N]$). Where $z = |y|^2$ with the complex
interference input signal $y$, and $\hat{\sigma}_i^{\,2}$ is the mean of the
interference power. The CA-CFAR threshold $T_{CA}$ is defined as the product of
the power estimate and the CA-CFAR constant $\alpha_{C\!A}$. $N$ represents the
total number of cells in the reference window.
\begin{gather*}
    T_{CA} = \alpha_{C\!A} \, \hat{\sigma}_i^2 \\
    \hat{\sigma}_i^2 = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N z_n \\
    \alpha_{CA} = N \left[{P_{F\!A}}^{\!\!-1/N} - 1\right]
\end{gather*}

\subsection{Object Tracking with the Kalman Filter}

\subsubsection{The Kalman Filter}

The Kalman filter is an optimal state estimation algorithm that models the
evolution of a system's state over time using a linear motion model. After
initialization it operates in a "predict-update" loop. In the predict step,
state and uncertainty are extrapolated based on current information and the
system motion. In the update step, the state estimate and uncertainty are
updated based on the system measurement. \\

\noindent \textbf{Predict} \\

\noindent 1. Extrapolate the state
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{\hat{x}}_{n+1,n} = \mathbf{F} \mathbf{\hat{x}}_{n,n} + \mathbf{G} \mathbf{u}_n
\end{equation*}
2. Extrapolate uncertainty
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{P}_{n+1,n} = \mathbf{F}\mathbf{P}_{n,n} \mathbf{F}^T + \mathbf{Q}
\end{equation*}

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
    \begin{tabular}{lp{8cm}}
        \toprule
        \midrule
        $\mathbf{\hat{x}}_{n,m}$ & Estimated system state vector at time step $n$, computed using information available at time $m$ \\
        $\mathbf{u}_n$ & Control variable or input variable, representing known external inputs to the system \\
        $\mathbf{F}$ & State transition matrix \\
        $\mathbf{G}$ & Input (control) matrix or input transition matrix, which maps inputs to state variables \\
        $\mathbf{P}_{n,m}$ & Covariance matrix (squared uncertainty) of state at time step $n$, computed using information available at time $m$ \\
        $\mathbf{Q}$ & Process noise matrix \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Kalman prediction system variables and parameters}
\end{table}

\noindent \textbf{Update} \\

\noindent 1. Compute the Kalman Gain
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{K}_n = \mathbf{P}_{n,n-1} \mathbf{H}^T \left(\mathbf{H}\mathbf{P}_{n,n-1}\mathbf{H}^T + \mathbf{R}\right)^{-1}
\end{equation*}
2. Update estimate with measurement
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{\hat{x}}_{n,n} = \mathbf{\hat{x}}_{n,n-1} + \mathbf{K}_n (\mathbf{z}_n - \mathbf{H}\mathbf{\hat{x}}_{n,n-1})
\end{equation*}
3. Update the estimate uncertainty
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{P}_{n,n} = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K}_n \mathbf{H}) \mathbf{P}_{n,n-1} (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{K}_n \mathbf{H})^T + \mathbf{K}_n \mathbf{R} \mathbf{K}_n^T
\end{equation*}

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.2}
    \begin{tabular}{lp{8cm}}
        \toprule
        \midrule
        $\mathbf{z}_n$ & Measurement vector \\
        $\mathbf{K}_n$ & Kalman gain \\
        $\mathbf{H}$ & Observation (measurement) matrix \\
        $\mathbf{R}$ & Measurement noise covariance matrix \\
        $\mathbf{I}$ & Identity matrix \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{Kalman update system variables and parameters}
\end{table}

\subsubsection{State Definition and Motion Model}

Define the state vector to include distance and velocity
\begin{equation*}
    \text{Let } \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} d \\ v \end{bmatrix}
\end{equation*}
Assuming constant velocity define the motion model state transition function
\begin{gather*}
    d_{k+1} = d_k + v_k \Delta t \quad , \quad v_{k+1} = v_k \\
    \implies \mathbf{F} = \begin{bmatrix}
        1 & \Delta t \\
        0 & 1
    \end{bmatrix}
\end{gather*}
CFAR measurements are directly in range and velocity therefore
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{H} = \mathbf{I}
\end{equation*}
The uncertainty of the motion model is defined using the standard formulation
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{Q} = \sigma_a^2 \begin{bmatrix}
        \frac{\Delta t^4}{4} & \frac{\Delta t^3}{2} \\
        \frac{\Delta t^3}{2} & \Delta t^2
    \end{bmatrix} \quad , \quad \text{where } \sigma_a^2 = \text{variance of unknown acceleration} \\
\end{equation*}
The uncertainty of the CFAR detections themselves (assuming no cross-correlation) is defined as
\begin{gather*}
    \mathbf{R} = \begin{bmatrix}
        \sigma_d^2 & 0 \\
        0 & \sigma_v^2
    \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{where} \quad
    \sigma_d \approx d_{res}/2 \quad , \quad \sigma_v \approx v_{res}/2
\end{gather*}
Initial conditions are reasonably defined as
\begin{gather*}
    \mathbf{x}_0 = \begin{bmatrix}d_{detected} \\ v_{detected}\end{bmatrix}
    \quad , \quad \mathbf{P}_0 = \mathbf{R}
\end{gather*}
There is no control input
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{u}_n = 0 \implies \mathbf{G} \text{ irrelevant}
\end{equation*}

\subsubsection{Data Association}

The Kalman update step accepts only one measurement ($z_n$) for each track. To
achieve this input a single CFAR detection must be associated with a specific
track. This is traditionally done trough gating; i.e. defining a region on the
range-Doppler map in which detections are associated with a track. The centroid
of detections inside the gate is then used as the track measurement.

Range gating is a rudimentary gating method where all detections within a
specified distance of the predicted range are selected. Finding the centroid of
gated detections produces a single usable input. The results of range gating
are shown below. Swerling II fluctuation greatly impacts the convergence time of
velocity tracking due to ambiguity in velocity (as shown in Figure
\ref{cfar:2d-fluctuating}).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/tracking-range-gated.png}
    \caption{Kalman ($\sigma_a = 0.1$) filter tracking results using a range gate (width $2
    d_{res}$), CA-CFAR detection, and Swerling II fluctuation}
\end{figure}

A more principled gating approach uses the Mahalanobis distance to define an
elliptical gate centered at the predicted state. A detection $\mathbf{z}$ is
included in the gate if:
\begin{equation}
    (\mathbf{z} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{n,n-1})^T \mathbf{S}^{-1}
    (\mathbf{z} - \hat{\mathbf{x}}_{n,n-1}) \leq \gamma
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{S} = H P_{n,n-1} H^T + R$ is the innovation covariance and
$\gamma$ is the gate threshold. Unlike range gating, this approach accounts
for uncertainty in both range and velocity, naturally excluding
Swerling-scattered detections that are statistically inconsistent with the
predicted state.


\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/tracking-mahalanobis.png}
    \caption{Kalman filter ($\sigma_a = 0.1$) tracking results using
    Mahalanobis gating with $\gamma = 9$, CA-CFAR detection, and Swerling II
    fluctuation}
\end{figure}

The results for Mahalanobis gating are shown above. The impact of Swerling II
fluctuation is wholly removed and both range and velocity track nearly
identically with the true values.
